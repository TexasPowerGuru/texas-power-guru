Texas Electricity

Simply enter your zip code in the plan finder. If plans appear, you're in a deregulated area. If your zip isn't found, you may be served by a co-op or municipal utility (like Austin Energy or CPS Energy in San Antonio), which are not part of the deregulated market and don't offer plan choice.

El Paso Electric and utilities serving certain rural areas also remain outside of deregulation.

Your TDU (Transmission and Distribution Utility) owns and maintains the power lines that deliver electricity to your home. TDU delivery charges are separate from your energy charge and are passed through on your bill — you cannot choose your TDU or avoid these charges.

The rates shown in Texas Power Guru are all-in rates — they already include TDU delivery charges as published on each plan's EFL. The cost you see is what you can expect to pay, not a pre-TDU number.

A bill credit plan offers a very low advertised rate (sometimes 5–7¢/kWh) but includes a catch: you only receive a credit on your bill if you use exactly a specific amount of electricity — typically 1,000 or 2,000 kWh in a month.

Use more or less than that threshold, and you lose the credit. Your effective rate can be two to three times higher than advertised at usage levels outside the threshold. These plans are specifically designed to be difficult to compare accurately.

Texas Power Guru filters these out entirely so that every plan you see has a transparent, predictable rate.

An early termination fee is a penalty charged if you cancel your electricity contract before it expires. ETFs on fixed-rate plans typically range from $0 (no penalty) to $150–$250 or more for flat-fee structures. Some plans charge a per-remaining-month fee instead.

You can sort plans by ETF in the plan finder, which is useful if you think there's any chance you'll move or switch before the contract ends.

Switching is typically seamless. There is no interruption to your electricity service — the same TDU continues to deliver power to your home. The only thing that changes is the company billing you for the energy.

Most switches take effect within one to two billing cycles. In some cases you can request an expedited switch date. Your new REP will coordinate the transition with your TDU automatically.

The Electricity Facts Label (EFL) is a one-page standardized disclosure required by the PUCT. When reviewing an EFL, pay attention to:

Rate at your actual usage level (not just at 1,000 kWh). Monthly recurring fees — some plans have base fees or service fees not captured in the per-kWh rate. The early termination fee and contract end date. Any special conditions — particularly language around bill credits or minimum usage requirements.

Every plan card in Texas Power Guru includes a direct link to that plan's EFL. Always read it before enrolling.

It's worth checking rates whenever your current contract is nearing its end — typically 30 to 60 days before the expiration date. Many contracts automatically roll to a month-to-month variable rate when they expire, which is usually more expensive.

Some people also shop during significant market shifts — such as after a large drop in natural gas prices — to see if locking in a new rate makes sense even if they have remaining contract time (factoring in any ETF).
